In our first example we are looking at the temperature reading (meta-data) associated with an experiment. For the experiment to work reliably, the temperature should be at around 12 Kelvin, and if we look at the data it is mostly consistent with 12 Kelvin to within the 0.4 degree precision of the thermometry and the thermal control system (standard deviation).

The above graph is a log-scale histogram of our contaminated data. We want to throw out the anomalous data from when the thermal control system was misbehaving or not working. The statistical question we seek to answer is "Which of these temperature data points are within 5σ of the background distribution?" (or equivalently, "Which data points are malfunctions or outliers with greater than 5σ certainty, which we will remove from our data?") and the answer is the data which we want to keep, as it is most likely real data that isn't contaminated with the anomalous readings.

In math,

Let $D$ be our clean data and $X$ be our plotted data.

$d = x$ $\forall$ $x$ where $F(x) > 5σ$, $F(x)$ being the cumulative distribution function and f(x) is the probability density function of our expected background.

In more specific terms, $$5\sigma < |\int_\infty^x f(x)dx|$$ has solutions $x_1, x_2$ where $x_1 \leq x_2$
$d \in D$, $\forall x_1 \leq d \leq x_2$
find $D$.

Once $D$ is found, a truth table is created to analyze the results.

X = np.sort(anomdat)
D = np.array([])
garb = np.array([])

def truthtable(sigma):
    sig = stats.norm.sf(sigma)
    maxTemp = stats.norm.isf(sig, loc=12, scale=0.4)
    minTemp = stats.norm.isf(1-sig, loc=12, scale=0.4)
    
    D = X[np.logical_and(X > minTemp, X < maxTemp)]
    garb = X[np.logical_or(X < minTemp, X > maxTemp)]
    print("Number of thrown values :", len(garb))
    print("Size of D:", len(D))

    truepos = sum((Counter(dat) & Counter(D)).values())
    falsepos = sum((Counter(D) & Counter(anom)).values())
    trueneg = sum((Counter(dat) & Counter(garb)).values())
    falseneg = sum((Counter(garb) & Counter(anom)).values())
    
    table = pd.DataFrame(data=[[truepos,falsepos],[trueneg,falseneg]],
                     index=["Pos", "Neg"],
                     columns=["True", "False"])
    return table

A potential asteroid would have true movement between the images; that is, there would be some data point with ($\Delta x$,$\Delta y$) displacement. The real distance is given by $$D = \sqrt{\Delta x^2 + \Delta y^2}$$

Given that both x and y are Gaussian distributed, the new distribution D is made by the square root of the sum of both squared Gaussian distributions. The distribution D then is a chi squared distribution with a degree of freedom of two, or a Rayleigh distribution. Because $\Delta x$ and $\Delta y$ have a standard deviation of one and mean of zero, the Rayleigh distribution has the default mean of $\sqrt{\frac{\pi}{2}}$ and variance of $\frac{4-\pi}{2}$.

The question we want to answer is "What distance would an asteroid have to travel (in arcseconds) to have 5σ certainty that it wasn't movement created by random noise from the atmospheric and instrumental effects?"

Restated in terms of math, what is $\tilde{d}$, the distance required to create a signal of 5 sigma certainty or greater? Given $5\sigma = 2.866e-07$
$$5\sigma \geq |\int_\infty^\tilde{d} f(\tilde{d})d\tilde{d}|$$
Solved by $\tilde{d} =$ rayleigh.ppf($1-5\sigma$)

Problem 3¶

Background¶

Cosmic rays are charged particles—usually protons or electrons but can include atomic nuclei such as alpha particles (helium) or iron. Because of their charge cosmic rays spiral in the magnetic field of the galaxy. From the perspective of the Earth they appear to be coming uniformly from all directions like a high energy gas, and the direction the cosmic ray is travelling when it reaches the Earth tells us nothing about where it came from because we don't know what tortured path it has taken through the galaxy to reach us. However, at trillion electron volt energies and above, the spiral loops are fairly big and the sun and the moon will block cosmic rays. This means the sun and the moon appear as holes in the cosmic ray sky (cosmic rays from that direction are absorbed).

Assume a moon sized patch on the sky we normally have a cosmic ray rate of 1 cosmic ray per minute (arrivals are random in time). If we can observe where the moon is for 8 hours per night (not too close to the horizon) and we observe for 15 days and see 6800 cosmic rays, what is the signficance of our moon shadow detection?

Analysis¶

In other words, what is the probability that we see 6800 cosmic rays over 15 days, coming from a background which produces 1 cosmic ray per minute? What is the significance (in sigma) of the signal, if there is one?

The background is discrete, given that cosmic rays come in integer amounts, therefore the probability mass function of a cosmic ray arriving for a given minute is most likely a Poisson distribution with the given average of one per minute. Summed over 15 days, 8 hours per day, comes to 7,200 samples, it will approach a Gaussian distribution by the central limit theorem. The trials factor is not particularly relevant as the source we are looking for is persistent, and one particularly unusual event in a sample won't be mistaken for a signal in this case; the relevant number is the sum of all samples.

Let $P$ be the Poisson distribution with a mean of $1$. Find the probability of a signal of strength $Y=6800$ over $N=7200$ samples, given a probability mass function $f(p)$.
